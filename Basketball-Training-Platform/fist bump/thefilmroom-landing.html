The Coaching Crisis

Youth sports are facing a crisis. Millions of volunteer coaches want to help kids, but they lack the training to do it effectively. And the athletes who need specialized coaching the most—neurodivergent kids with ADHD, autism, and anxiety—have virtually no resources.

Untrained Coaches

Fewer than 20% of youth coaches are trained in effective motivational techniques. Only 33% know sport skills and tactics. Less than one-third understand basic safety protocols.

Kids Quitting

70% of kids quit sports by age 13. Children with trained coaches show only 5% attrition vs. 26% with untrained coaches.

Neurodivergent Gap

Over 11% of youth athletes have ADHD or autism. Yet there are no formal certifications for coaching these athletes. Zero.

Wrong Approach

Traditional coaching focuses on what players do wrong. Research shows 80-90% positive reinforcement drives better performance. But coaches don't know how to implement it.

"I got it wrong with my own son.

When Jackson was a youth player, I held him to a different standard than my other players. I pushed harder. Demanded more. And the harder I pushed, the worse he played.

I wasn't Bobby Knight. I wasn't abusive. But I was wrong.

As my coaching evolved—as I learned to celebrate the small things, to laugh more, to focus on what he was doing right—he flourished. Not just as a player. As a person.

That evolution in my coaching style changed everything. And years later, I found out that a Stanley Cup championship team had data proving why it worked."

— Derek Simmons, Module 1 Instructor
