# Teaching Moment: You Don't Need Another Trophy

**Date:** February 12, 2026
**Context:** 5th-6th grade boys rec league, final week of season
**Status:** Active — tournament week

---

## The Situation

Season-long pattern of disrespect. Not competitive fire — flat-out disrespect. Three of five point guards can't figure it out. The team is the 1-seed heading into the season-ending tournament. They should win and it shouldn't be close. But it will be.

This isn't hard basketball. This is one of the most embarrassing coaching jobs in 30+ years — not because of talent, but because effort and respect can't be coached into kids who don't want it.

---

## The Plan

Two practices left. Split the gym.

**One end:** The three who can't figure it out. Shoot. Sleep. Don't care. But if you mess around, you're out of the gym. Done done. League says equal playing time — paid your money. Says nothing about practice.

**Other end:** The seven who give a shit and want to play hard and play together. If any of the seven slide, they move down.

No speeches. No second chances. No negotiation. Consequences moved from words to geography.

---

## The Framework

**Trophies vs. Teammates.**

If they lose the tournament and say "we didn't practice the last week like we should have" — the answer is: you learn some lessons the hard way. You don't need another trophy. You need to figure out how to be a better teammate.

Low-stakes basketball. High-stakes character. The trophy doesn't teach anything. The consequence might.

---

## Coaching Implications

- You can't coach effort and respect into kids who don't want it. Stop trying.
- Protect the kids who earned it. The seven who show up right deserve a real practice.
- Consequences don't require yelling. Separation is louder than a lecture.
- Equal playing time is a league rule. Practice is yours.
- When you've tried everything and it hasn't worked, the last move is to stop trying to fix it and let the natural consequence land.

---

## The Test

Did the seven get better this week? That's the only question that matters. The three made their choice. The seven didn't — and they shouldn't pay for it.
